Wheatstone Bridge Overview

The Wheatstone bridge is a foundational analog circuit for measuring small resistance changes. In this miniPCB™, it is configured for ratiometric sensing, commonly used with pressure sensors, strain gauges, and other variable resistive elements.

Bridge Configuration

  • R3 and R5 form the negative leg (N leg)
  • R4 and R6 form the positive leg (P leg)
  • R5 is typically populated with the sensor element whose resistance varies with pressure
  • R1 and R2 are optional and can be used to limit or set the bridge’s bias and excitation point

The bridge is powered by an excitation voltage applied across the top and bottom of the diamond, and the differential output is taken from the midpoint of each leg. When balanced, the output is zero. When the sensor element (e.g., R5) changes resistance, the output deviates from zero proportionally.

Design Considerations

  • Sensor location: Place the variable resistor (sensor) in R5 or R6 for highest sensitivity
  • Initial balance: Use matched resistors (e.g., R3 ≈ R4, R6 ≈ nominal R5)
  • Excitation voltage: Stable and low-noise — often regulated or DAC-controlled
  • Output impedance: Should be connected to a high-impedance input (e.g., instrumentation amplifier)
  • Temperature drift: Minimized using resistors with low tempco and symmetric layout

Educational Value

  • Observe how bridge imbalance produces a measurable voltage
  • Explore sensor placement and sensitivity
  • Demonstrate the importance of resistor matching and layout symmetry
  • Visualize common-mode vs. differential signal behavior

Stability Notes – Phase Margin and Gain Margin

This circuit includes an instrumentation amplifier implemented using three op-amps from an LM324 quad package. Like all feedback systems, stability is influenced by the behavior of the closed-loop gain at different frequencies. Two key indicators of system stability are phase margin and gain margin.

Phase Margin

Phase margin is the amount of additional phase shift required to bring the loop gain phase to –180° at the frequency where the gain is unity (0 dB). It serves as a measure of how close the system is to oscillation. A phase margin of:

  • ≥ 45° is generally considered stable
  • 20°–45° may cause ringing or overshoot
  • < 20° risks sustained oscillation

In this design, the 10 nF capacitor (C1) in the feedback loop improves phase margin by reducing high-frequency gain, which helps prevent oscillation and noise amplification — especially when driving an ADC or capacitive load.

Gain Margin

Gain margin is the amount by which loop gain can increase before reaching unity gain at a phase of –180°. A typical safe range is:

  • ≥ 10 dB is preferred

While gain margin is less commonly adjusted directly in this type of analog signal chain, the use of reasonable resistor values and moderate open-loop gain from the LM324 op-amps contributes to a safe margin.

Practical Impact

  • Improved phase margin leads to smoother step responses and more predictable transient behavior
  • Excessive bandwidth can introduce noise or instability, especially with long wires or unshielded sensors
  • C1 = 10 nF intentionally limits bandwidth to ~160 Hz for low-noise, slow-signal applications like pressure sensing

Summary

This miniPCB™ includes intentional low-pass filtering to improve phase margin and ensure stability, making it suitable for high-accuracy, low-speed sensing applications. For applications requiring faster response, the value of C1 may be reduced — at the cost of reduced phase margin and increased sensitivity to layout and load conditions.

Circuit Description

1. Power Supply Conditioning (Left side, P1-1 to P1-3)

  • U2 (LM7810): 10 V linear regulator fed from V+ at P1-1; provides a regulated supply for the Wheatstone bridge and amplifiers.
  • C8, C9, C6, C7: Input/output filter capacitors for the regulator.
  • TP1, TP2, TP3: Test points to monitor regulator input, output, and bridge supply.

Purpose: Deliver a clean, stable excitation voltage for the sensor bridge.

2. Wheatstone Bridge (Middle-left)

  • R3, R4, R5, R6 form the bridge network.
  • R5: Slot for a negative coefficient sensor (e.g., NTC thermistor).
  • R6: Slot for a positive coefficient sensor (e.g., PTC thermistor or strain gauge).
  • C10, C11: Likely noise suppression across bridge legs.
  • Outputs: Labeled P (positive) and N (negative) feeding the instrumentation amplifier.

Functions

  • Sensor excitation (from regulated voltage).
  • Signal extraction — produces a small differential voltage proportional to sensor imbalance.

3. Instrumentation Amplifier (Middle-right)

  • Built from LM324 op amps.
  • U1A and U1B: First-stage unity-gain buffers for each bridge output (high input impedance).
  • U1C: Differential amplifier stage removing common-mode voltage and amplifying the difference.
  • R7, R8, R12, R13: Set amplifier gain and balance.
  • C1: Optional filtering at the differential stage for high-frequency noise.
  • U1D: Output buffer/driver.
  • TP11, TP12, TP4: Test points for stage verification.

4. Outputs

  • P1-6: Amplified differential output (measurement signal).
  • P1-7: Reference or buffered excitation voltage for downstream electronics (as applicable).

5. Notes in Title Block

  • Typical sensor elements can have negative or positive temperature coefficients.
  • The Wheatstone bridge both excites the sensor and extracts its signal.
  • The instrumentation amplifier provides amplification and common-mode rejection.

How It Works Overall

  1. Power regulation supplies a stable 10 V to the bridge and amplifier.
  2. The Wheatstone bridge generates a small differential voltage from sensor resistance changes.
  3. The instrumentation amplifier:
    • Buffers each bridge output,
    • Removes common-mode voltage,
    • Amplifies the small differential signal.
  4. The result is a clean, amplified output proportional to the measured parameter.
