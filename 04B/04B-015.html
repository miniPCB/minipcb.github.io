AI Generated

The 04B-015 implements a complementary push–pull emitter follower biased toward Class‑AB by R1, R2, D1, and D2. C2 and C3 provide local rail decoupling. TP1 monitors the raw input, C1 AC‑couples that input into the bias network midpoint observed at TP2. The transistor pair output (before coupling) is at TP3. C4 AC‑couples the amplifier to the load R3, and the AC‑coupled output is available at TP5. TP4 monitors V+.

Typical coupling choices: C1 ≈ 10 nF to 1 µF depending on the source impedance. Larger C1 lowers the high‑pass corner (fc ≈ 1/(2π·Rin·C1)) for better low‑frequency response but increases charge transients and physical size; smaller values reduce turn‑on thumps and recovery times at the expense of bass extension. Bias network resistors R1/R2 are commonly 10–100 kΩ. Lower values improve bias stiffness and noise immunity (and reduce crossover distortion stability issues) but raise quiescent current; higher values save power but increase susceptibility to drift and hum. D1/D2 are typically small‑signal silicon diodes (e.g., ~0.6–0.7 V each). Tighter thermal coupling to Q1/Q2 improves tracking; higher forward drop reduces crossover distortion but increases idle current and heat.

On the output, C4 is usually 47–470 µF. Larger values keep fc low with typical loads (R3 || external), but increase size and start‑up transients; smaller values are compact and fast to settle but attenuate low frequencies. R3 is often 330 Ω to 2.2 kΩ for bench loading: low R3 reveals current capability and crossover behavior at TP3/TP5 but raises dissipation; high R3 eases loading and highlights small‑signal linearity.

  • C2/C3: 100 nF ceramic close to Q1/Q2 plus 10–100 µF bulk reduces rail ripple and prevents oscillation.
  • Expect ~1.2–1.4 V total bias across D1+D2 at room temperature.
  • Compare TP3 vs TP5 to see the effect of C4’s high‑pass on low‑frequency amplitude and phase.
  • Use short leads and a solid ground reference when probing TP2/TP3 to avoid injecting hum into the bias node.
